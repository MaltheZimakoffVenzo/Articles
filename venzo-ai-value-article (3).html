AI Implementation

Why Companies Can't Make AI Value Stick

And how the organizations that succeed are redesigning work instead of automating it.

March 2026
Malthe Zimakoff Change, AI & Strategy at VENZO
Andreas Mogensen Head of Change at VENZO
A Pattern That Predates AI

When companies today struggle with getting value out of their AI investments, it is far from a new problem. In 1987, the economist Robert Solow noticed something strange. America had spent billions on computers. Yet none of it was showing up in economic growth.

When factories first electrified in the 1880s, they swapped in electric motors and left everything else the same. For forty years, the productivity data barely moved at all. The value only appeared when a new generation of owners redesigned the factory itself: open floor plans, assembly lines, new roles, new skills. The result was not a more efficient version of the old factory. It was a different kind of factory entirely.

AI is in the electric-motor-swap phase right now, except the cycle that took electricity forty years is compressing into years, or even months. In early 2026, a survey of thousands of CEOs found no measurable AI impact on productivity. The largest enterprise Copilot evaluation to date (20,000 employees, three months) reported significant user-reported time savings, but the report itself admitted it could not identify how that saved time was actually spent.

Promising cases exist. Klarna's AI assistant handled 2.3 million customer conversations in a single month, doing the equivalent work of 700 agents. But even that is one task, in one function, designed for automation.

The question is not whether AI can make individuals more efficient. It is why that value evaporates on the way to a business outcome.

There is real value in AI-assisted productivity. Nobody disputes that. But there is a ceiling to what can be achieved by adding AI to existing workflows. Beyond that ceiling, the work itself has to change.

As with electricity, capturing the value requires building new organizational capabilities. That starts with redesigning work end-to-end: from what AI automates, through the behavioral change each role needs to make, to the new behaviors required for freed capacity to create value. If any link in that chain is undesigned, value leaks out.

Three Conditions

The Three Conditions Required for AI Value Realization

Across AI programs, the same three behavioral conditions separate organizations that create real value from those that do not. The AI must improve the process. People must adopt new ways of working in a meaningful way. And the value must land somewhere strategically valuable rather than dissipate.

CONDITION 01

Are you automating the right work?

AI must improve the process

Before asking whether the AI output is good enough, there is a prior question: does this automation remove a real constraint? A strategy consultant uses AI to produce 30-page market analyses instead of 10. But the client never read past page 5. The bottleneck was the consultant's ability to distill complexity into a recommendation, not produce more of it. A finance team generates weekly dashboards that used to take a month. But the organization was already drowning in reports nobody acted on. The constraint was getting a decision-maker to spend 20 minutes with the data and make a call. Faster reports just pile up at the same downstream bottleneck.

And even when AI removes a genuine bottleneck and saves real time, the value is never in the task it performed. It is in what becomes possible because that task is done. A dishwasher's value is not clean dishes. It is the hour the chef got back. But the restaurant only benefits if that hour goes toward a better menu, not more of the same dishes.

CONDITION 02

Your highest-skilled people are your lowest adopters

People must adopt new ways of working

Here is where things become hard. The people best positioned to validate AI output, the senior practitioners, the domain experts, the people with fifteen years of experience, are consistently the lowest adopters. The resistance makes sense. A senior analyst watching AI generate a financial model in seconds is watching the market value of their core skill collapse in real time. Research on social threat (Rock, 2008) explains why AI hits harder than previous technologies: it can simultaneously threaten how people feel about their status, certainty, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness. Most technology changes trigger one of these. AI can trigger all five at once.

Most organizations treat the identity threat as a problem to manage. Research on organizational change suggests the opposite: treat it as intelligence. Help people see where their value is going, not where it is disappearing. That senior analyst's new value is knowing whether the model is right, what it means for the business, and what to do about it.

Judgment here is not abstract. It is knowing what the AI was never told: the client relationship, the regulatory shift, the reason last quarter was an anomaly. It is spotting confident outputs that would quietly embarrass the business. And it is turning a model's answer into a recommendation someone can act on.

You cannot make someone a good reviewer by telling them to review. Being excellent at building a financial model does not prepare someone for critically evaluating one. Zuboff (1988) studied this exact transition in earlier waves of automation: organizations that invested in building the judgment capability produced more valuable workers. Those that just automated and expected people to figure it out produced worse business outcomes.

The judgment skill must be practiced, not explained. That means structured sessions where practitioners evaluate AI-generated work alongside their own, in settings where getting it wrong is safe. Pilot projects where people try the new role before it becomes a mandate (Ibarra, 1999).

The scale of resistance is proportional to the identity shift demanded. Mandating adoption tells people the organization sees them as an obstacle. Investing in their development tells them it sees a future worth building toward. Structured learning time, mentorship around new capabilities, incentives for skill growth: these are not perks. They are trust signals. And without them, no AI program survives first contact with the people who have to make it work.

CONDITION 03

The forty minutes nobody can account for

Freed capacity must go somewhere strategic

It's a Tuesday afternoon. Using AI, a marketing analyst finishes a campaign report in twenty minutes instead of the usual hour. Nothing is designed to happen next. So the freed time goes to email, a few messages, a meeting that could have been a Teams message. By five o'clock, the freed forty minutes have vanished.

This is not because people lack the discipline. It is a failure of design. When technology removes a task from someone's day, it removes the prompt for one behavior without supplying a prompt for the next.

Two factors are responsible for the freed-up time disappearing:

First there is Jevons Paradox: when something becomes more efficient, people consume more of it. AI drafts emails in seconds, so people send more emails, which generates more emails for everyone else to respond to. AI makes it easy to run analyses, so teams produce more analyses without improving a single decision. AI generates presentations faster, so more decks get created, which means more meetings to present them and more people pulled in to review them. The efficiency gain becomes volume increase.

The second is Parkinson's Law: work expands to fill the time available. AI generates a first draft in minutes. Instead of shipping it, the person spends an hour refining because the time exists. The draft was good enough. The extra polish adds nothing the recipient will notice. AI produces an analysis in seconds. Instead of making the decision, the team requests three more scenarios because they're now cheap to produce. The task that was supposed to take an hour still takes an hour.

Both consume the freed capacity without producing actual business value. And both are preventable, but only through deliberate design. Without a pre-designed answer to "what do I do now?", people will either produce more of what they already know how to produce, or default into whatever is familiar. How hard it is to fill that vacuum depends on the distance between the old task and the desired new one. And for any given team, that distance is different for every role.

The Friction Map

Why the Same AI Deployment Creates Three Different Problems

A single AI deployment on a single team creates all three friction levels at once. The junior developer's transition from routine coding to AI-assisted coding might be low friction. The mid-level developer's transition from writing to reviewing AI output is medium friction, requiring a cognitive architecture they may never have practiced. The senior architect's transition from production to pure judgment and system design is high friction, with real identity-level resistance.

When organizations look at the team as a system, something else becomes visible. Not just where value evaporates, but which new configurations become possible. Maybe junior developers absorb review tasks that senior architects used to handle, tasks they can manage now because AI assists their judgment. This frees the architects for system design work that was never possible before, because they were permanently buried in review queues. The value is not in any individual's freed hours, it is in the new configuration of the team, one that produces capabilities the organization did not have before.

The Missing Piece

The Measurement Gap

Most AI business cases start from the tool: "Copilot saves developers five hours per week." That is a statement about a technology with no connection to a business outcome.

An honest business case starts from the capability, not the tool. Not "Copilot saves five hours per week" but "We need a 30% reduction in legacy system incidents over twelve months. What does AI need to deliver, what behaviors need to change, and where does freed capacity need to go to make that happen?"

That requires knowing whether behaviors actually changed, not just whether the tool was used. Traditional metrics cannot tell you this. They track activity: logins, clicks, sessions. But the question is behavioral: did someone start their work from the AI draft instead of a blank page? Did freed capacity go to strategic work or dissolve into email? AI interaction data can reveal this at a level that was never possible before. Turning it into a diagnostic requires investment, but the signal has never been more granular.

The problem with self-reporting, and other shallow metrics, is not just imprecision. It can be backwards. METR's 2025 trial found developers who believed they were 20% faster with AI were actually 19% slower. With AI interaction data we can do better, but turning it into a role-level diagnostic requires joining it with org data and workflow systems. The infrastructure is what most organizations still have to build.

One thing is sure: simply checking whether the tool is being actively used, often defined as one interaction per month, tells an organization nothing about whether work has changed or where freed capacity ended up.

The Lesson from Electricity

Build the muscle now

Better measurement reveals the gap. It does not close it.

The productivity paradox was never solved just by better technology. It was solved by the organizations that did something harder: they redesigned work itself, the roles, the flows, the skills, the meaning of expertise, and built capabilities the old system could never have supported.

The organizations building the reallocation muscle today are not just capturing more from their current AI investment. They are building the capability to capture more from every investment that follows.
